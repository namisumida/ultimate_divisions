2. Offensive points

In all three divisions, the winning teams converted about eight-in-ten of all of their offensive points: 80% for Mixed, 78% for Men's, and 78% for Women's - in-line with expectations for most elite club teams.

While a large majority of these points were holds, a sizable portion of them were not without turnovers. For the winning teams in the Women’s and Mixed divisions, about four-in-ten of all offensive points were single-possession scores (i.e. without turnovers). In contrast, the winning teams in the Men’s division stand out, having scored 56% of their offensive points in one possession.

To look at offensive efficiency for individual games, check out this interactive visualization.

It is important to note that the chart above only shows metrics for the winning teams. The winning teams in the Women's division only include the top four teams: Boston Brute Squad (4), San Francisco Fury (3), Seattle Riot (2), and Columbia Revolution. Similarly, only four teams are represented in the Mixed division: Seattle Mixtape (4), Boston Slow White (3), Philadelphia AMP (2), and Metro North. In the Men's division, however, there is a somewhat wider spread of teams: San Francisco Revolver (4), Boston Ironside (2), Chicago Machine, New York PoNY, Austin Doublewide, and Seattle Sockeye.

While winning teams clearly have more offensive holds than losing teams, it is not necessarily the case that their offensive holds are more likely to be single-possession scores. In fact, a range of differences emerges between the two types of teams in how often they score in a single possession. However, losing teams are already less likely than winning teams to score at all. Therefore, if we compare the share of offensive points that had single-possession scores, losing teams are automatically at a disadvantage. So instead, we must change the base from “all offensive points” to “all offensive holds”. Whereas previously we asked, “How often do teams score in a single possession?” we now ask: “When teams do score, how often are they in one possession?”

In the Women’s division, the winning and losing teams had the same share of offensive holds that were scored without turnovers, at around 60%. In the Men’s division, the winning teams - mainly Revolver - were not only more likely to score, but to do so with single-possession scores - by 17 percentage-points. The Mixed division, on the other hand, had losing teams holding with single-possession scores more often than winning teams by 12 points. Therefore, the winning teams - mainly Mixtape - were more likely than losing teams to score their offensive points, but not without error.

It’s impossible to tell whether these differences in the Men’s and Mixed divisions are real differences between teams that win and teams that lose, or instead are characteristic differences between Revolver/Mixtape and other teams. However, the similarity found in the Women’s division - in which no single team dominates wins/losses - may imply the latter.
